# First Principles Thinking

> Break a problem down to its fundamental truths, then reason up from there — rather than reasoning by analogy from what others have done.

Made famous by Elon Musk, but the method is ancient (Aristotle). The core move: refuse to accept inherited assumptions. Keep asking "why?" until you hit something that can't be reduced further.

## The method

1. **Identify the problem** you're trying to solve
2. **List all your assumptions** about it
3. **Question each assumption** — "Is this actually true, or is it just how it's always been done?"
4. **Rebuild** from the truths that remain

## Why it's hard

Reasoning by analogy is faster and usually good enough. "Other successful subscription boxes do X, so we should do X." First principles takes more effort. Save it for the decisions that matter most.

## Where it generates the most value

First principles thinking is most powerful when:
- Everyone in an industry does it one way (legacy assumption hiding an opportunity)
- You're entering a new domain and don't know the "rules" yet
- Costs seem fixed but might not be (space, materials, distribution)

## Cross-domain application

The same principle applies to personal decisions. Instead of "how do most people build wealth?" ask: "what is wealth fundamentally, and what are the actual levers that produce it?"

This connects directly to [[Concepts/Opportunity Cost]] — first principles thinking helps you see the real alternatives, not just the conventional ones.
